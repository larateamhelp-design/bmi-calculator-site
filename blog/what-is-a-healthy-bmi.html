What Is a Healthy BMI? A Simple Guide for Adults

Updated May 2026 · 5 min read

BMI — Body Mass Index — is one of the most widely used tools for assessing whether a person's weight is in a healthy range relative to their height. While it has limitations, it remains a useful first screening measure used by doctors, researchers, and health organisations worldwide.

What Is a Healthy BMI Range?

For most adults aged 18 and over, the World Health Organization (WHO) defines BMI categories as follows:

BMI RangeCategory
Below 18.5Underweight
18.5 – 24.9Healthy weight
25.0 – 29.9Overweight
30.0 – 34.9Obese Class I
35.0 – 39.9Obese Class II
40.0 and aboveObese Class III

So a healthy BMI is generally considered to be anywhere between 18.5 and 24.9. This applies to both men and women, though body composition can differ between the sexes.

How Is BMI Calculated?

BMI is calculated using a simple formula:

  • Metric: BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height (m)²
  • Imperial: BMI = (weight (lbs) ÷ height (inches)²) × 703

For example, a person who weighs 70 kg and is 175 cm tall has a BMI of 70 ÷ (1.75)² = 22.9, which falls in the healthy range.

Is a Healthy BMI Different for Men and Women?

The BMI formula is the same for men and women. However, because women naturally carry more body fat than men at the same BMI, some researchers suggest slightly different interpretations. In practice, most clinical guidelines use the same 18.5–24.9 healthy range for all adults.

Does BMI Account for Age?

The standard adult BMI scale does not adjust for age. However, older adults (65+) may have a slightly higher healthy BMI because some extra weight can be protective against bone loss and illness. For children and teenagers, a separate age- and sex-specific BMI percentile scale is used.

Limitations of BMI

BMI is a useful screening tool, but it has well-known limitations:

  • Athletes and muscular people may have a high BMI despite low body fat.
  • Older adults may have a normal BMI but low muscle mass.
  • Ethnicity can affect how BMI relates to health risk — some guidelines use lower thresholds for people of Asian descent.
  • BMI does not measure where fat is stored — abdominal fat carries more health risk than fat elsewhere.

Always use BMI alongside other measures like waist circumference, blood pressure, and blood tests for a fuller picture of health.

Summary

A healthy BMI for most adults is between 18.5 and 24.9. It's a quick, free, and easy screening tool — but not a complete health diagnosis. Use it as a starting point, then speak with a healthcare professional if you have concerns about your weight or health.
